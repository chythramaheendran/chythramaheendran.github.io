Enterprise SaaS GenAI Knowledge Mgmt
HashedIn by Deloitte · 2022–Present

Artefact Management System — making the organisation's knowledge actually findable

The Problem

The organisation's offering portfolios, sector content, and reusable artefacts lived across disconnected tools. Teams were duplicating work because they couldn't find what already existed. The real problem wasn't storage — it was discoverability at scale.

Discovery
  • Ran stakeholder feedback sessions across 6 business units to map how people searched for content
  • Identified that 70% of searches returned no results — not because content was missing, but because metadata was inconsistent
  • Benchmarked internal knowledge tools at comparable consulting firms
What I Did
  • Owned the full roadmap — prioritising metadata standardisation before layering AI search on top
  • Shipped AI conversational search alongside structured metadata search and role-based governance
  • Delivered 5+ enterprise releases replacing manual configuration with self-service workflows
Outcome
  • 30% improvement in document-retrieval efficiency across the knowledge base
  • ~25% improvement in operational efficiency through automated, self-service workflows
What I'd do differently

I'd invest earlier in a content governance ritual — not just the tooling. We fixed discoverability but didn't fully solve the upstream problem of teams not tagging content correctly at the point of creation. A lightweight checklist built into the upload flow would have compounded the gains faster.

+ 20% faster deployments
GenAI Automation DevOps Platform
HashedIn by Deloitte · 2022–Present

DevOps Assist — automating CI/CD pipelines and infrastructure-as-code with GenAI

The Problem

Engineers were spending significant time hand-writing Terraform configurations and CI/CD pipelines — repetitive, error-prone work that slowed delivery and required specialist knowledge. Pipeline migration in particular had no good automation story.

Discovery
  • Interviewed 12 engineers across 4 delivery teams to map the exact friction points in pipeline setup
  • Identified that migration — not initial setup — was the most time-consuming bottleneck
  • Evaluated existing internal tooling and external solutions; none handled migration without manual intervention
What I Did
  • Led the GenAI automation plugin end to end — owning roadmap, backlog, and release notes across 10+ releases
  • Scoped and shipped 5 AI capabilities including automated Terraform generation and CI/CD pipeline migration
  • Built a phased enablement programme: documentation and user guides shipped alongside every release
Outcome
  • Deployment workflows accelerated by ~20%
  • 85% self-service adoption — teams stopped raising support tickets for pipeline setup
What I'd do differently

The enablement strategy came after the first two releases, not before. Adoption lagged in the first month because engineers didn't trust the generated output without seeing it validated against a real project. I'd now ship a reference implementation first — before any general-use release.

Personal Project Firebase Full-stack Mobile + Web
Personal Project · Jul 2025–Present

SpendSync — from a raw Excel file to a live shared expense tracker

The Problem

10 months of household expense data lived in a spreadsheet — tracked meticulously but not usable. No simultaneous editing, no mobile view, no settlement tracking, no real-time sync. I also wanted to understand what it actually means to ship a product using AI as a development partner.

Process
  • Extracted and reconciled 750 transactions across 12 Excel sheets — fixing date serials, null categories, and refund entries — matching all 10 months to the rupee
  • Designed and built the full dashboard: charts, settlement calculator, 5-trip breakdown, collapsible transaction list
  • Integrated Firebase Firestore for real-time sync and Firebase Auth for Google sign-in; built role-based access (Owner / Editor / Viewer) by email
  • Debugged mobile-specific issues: sheet overflow, tab switching bug, Android OAuth state partitioning, desktop layout failure at 100% zoom
Outcome
  • Live at spendsync-56290.web.app — works on Android, iOS, and desktop
  • ₹5,78,577 tracked, real-time sync across devices, ₹0/month running cost
  • Entire app in a single 157KB HTML file — no build step, no dependencies
What I learned

A PM who can ship and debug — even with AI doing the coding — thinks about products differently. Understanding why a CSS breakpoint was breaking the desktop layout, or why signInWithRedirect fails on Android, changes how I write requirements and review engineering decisions. AI is a capable pair programmer, but the product judgement — what to build, in what order, and why — still has to come from a human.

Product Philosophy

How I think about product

Four beliefs I've arrived at across enterprise SaaS, GenAI tooling, and building things myself.

01
Diagnose before you prescribe
The stated problem is rarely the real problem. I don't move to solution space until I've talked to the people experiencing the friction and looked at the data behind their experience. On AMS, engineers said "search doesn't work." The actual problem was that metadata was inconsistent at upload time. Those have completely different solutions.
02
Adoption is a product problem, not a comms problem
If users aren't adopting something you shipped, the instinct is to communicate more. Usually the answer is to reduce friction in the product itself. On DevOps Assist, adoption lagged until I added a reference implementation that showed the output was trustworthy — not because we marketed it better, but because we removed the uncertainty.
03
Engineering trust is earned through specificity
Vague requirements produce vague software. I write PRDs that are specific about the why, precise about acceptance criteria, and honest about trade-offs. Having an engineering background means I know what makes a requirement easy to implement versus ambiguous — and I try to close that gap before a sprint starts, not during it.
04
Ship something, then make it right
Perfect roadmaps for products that haven't shipped yet are fiction. I bias towards getting a version in front of real users, measuring what actually happens, and iterating. The 85% self-service adoption on DevOps Assist came from 10+ releases — each one informed by how engineers used the previous one. You can't plan your way to that.
